History and Theory of Roman Numerals

Roman numerals originated in ancient Rome and were used throughout the Roman Empire in various contexts, including numbering years, counting money, and indicating the order of individuals in a sequence.

The basic symbols in the Roman numeral system are:

  • I - 1
  • V - 5
  • X - 10
  • L - 50
  • C - 100
  • D - 500
  • M - 1000

Roman numerals follow certain rules for representing numbers:

  • Repeated symbols represent addition. For example, II represents 1 + 1 = 2.
  • When a smaller numeral precedes a larger one, it represents subtraction. For example, IV represents 5 - 1 = 4.
  • When a smaller numeral follows a larger one, it represents addition. For example, VI represents 5 + 1 = 6.
  • There are no Roman numerals for zero or negative numbers.

Despite the decline of the Roman Empire, Roman numerals are still used today in various contexts, such as indicating the year on building inscriptions, numbering chapters in books, and denoting the order of sporting events.
